## Large Language Models (LLMs)

---

**language model** 

It is a system that takes a prompt as input and generates a completion by repeatedly predicting the next most likely token.

When given a prompt like "I try to learn something new," the model doesn't just pick one word with certainty. Instead, it assigns probabilities to all possible next tokens. For example, "every" might be the most likely, but "each," "everyday," or even a new line could also be possibilities. The model essentially "spins a wheel" weighted by those probabilities to pick the next token, then repeats the process until the output is complete.

Because the output is built from probabilistic predictions, it's not guaranteed to always be accurate.

---

**Why "large"?**

To handle this incredibly complex task well, these models are trained on massive amounts of data, including a large portion of the crawlable web and extensive literature. This is what makes them "large" language models.

After initial training, LLMs are often **fine tuned** to make them more conversational or to improve performance on specific tasks.

---

**Things to Remember:** 

LLMs generate text token by token, each step based on the probability of what comes next given everything before it. The quality of the output depends heavily on both the training data and any fine-tuning applied.
